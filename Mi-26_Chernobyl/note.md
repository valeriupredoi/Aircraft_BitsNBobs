## The Mi-26 helicopter at Chernobyl: role, units involved, and final fate

The **Mil Mi-26 “Halo”** became one of the most important heavy-lift helicopters used during the Soviet response to the **Chernobyl nuclear disaster of April 1986**. The accident destroyed **Reactor No. 4** at the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant, releasing radioactive material and creating an urgent need to suppress the fire, reduce contamination, and construct a protective enclosure. ([Nuclear Regulatory Commission][1])

The Mi-26 was chosen because of its extraordinary lifting capability. At the time it was the world’s largest production helicopter, capable of carrying loads far beyond smaller Soviet helicopters such as the Mi-8 and Mi-6. A special emergency variant, the **Mi-26S**, was adapted for Chernobyl duties with radiation-protection measures, spraying equipment, and systems for delivering materials near the damaged reactor. ([Wikipedia][2])

---

## Mission at Chernobyl

The Mi-26s performed several key tasks:

### 1. Dropping containment materials over Reactor 4

In the first days after the explosion, helicopters flew repeated missions over the open reactor core, dropping materials intended to reduce radioactive emissions and smother the burning remains. Early helicopter operations involved mixtures including sand, clay, boron compounds, lead, and other materials. Boron was intended to help prevent further nuclear reactions, while heavy materials helped shield and cover the exposed fuel and debris. ([Nuclear Regulatory Commission][1])

The Mi-26s were particularly useful later in the operation because they could:

* carry large loads of construction material;
* deliver equipment close to the reactor;
* spray liquid decontamination compounds;
* perform radiation-monitoring flights.

Approximately **30 Mi-26 helicopters** were involved in Chernobyl-related operations, although not all were simultaneously committed to reactor-covering missions. ([Wikipedia][2])

---

## Units and organisations involved

The helicopters were operated by Soviet military aviation units assigned to the Chernobyl liquidation effort. The main aviation force came from the:

* **Soviet Air Force (VVS)** helicopter regiments;
* **Military Transport Aviation support structures**;
* specialist aviation detachments brought in from across the USSR.

One of the best-known groups involved was the **336th Separate Helicopter Regiment**, which supplied Mi-26 crews for the operation. Other Mi-26s were brought from different Soviet bases as the scale of the emergency became clear. Sources from participants describe multiple aircraft and crews operating from staging areas away from the reactor, including the area around **Gomel and Kyiv-region airfields**. ([Reddit][3])

The crews were supported by:

* radiation-control teams;
* chemical protection troops;
* engineers preparing loads and equipment;
* ground crews maintaining aircraft under contaminated conditions.

Flight crews faced severe exposure risks. Aircraft had to operate directly above or near the destroyed reactor, and missions were carefully limited because radiation doses accumulated quickly. ([Reddit][3])

---

## The Mi-26 crash often associated with Chernobyl

A common misconception is that an Mi-26 crashed at Chernobyl. The famous helicopter crash was actually an **Mi-8**, which crashed on 2 October 1986 after striking a crane while performing decontamination work. All four crew members were killed. ([Wikipedia][4])

However, Mi-26 aircraft were also heavily contaminated and later became part of the abandoned equipment left behind after the cleanup.

---

## What happened to the Mi-26 helicopters afterwards?

Unlike ordinary aircraft, the Chernobyl Mi-26s could not simply return to normal service without consideration of contamination.

### Some were decontaminated and returned to service

Aircraft that received lower radiation doses were cleaned, inspected, and returned to Soviet aviation units. Decontamination procedures included washing, removing contaminated components, and monitoring radiation levels.

### Others were abandoned or stored

Several heavily contaminated helicopters were sent to storage areas and equipment graveyards associated with the Chernobyl exclusion zone. The most famous was the **Rassokha vehicle graveyard**, where many contaminated vehicles, including large helicopters, were stored. Over the years, valuable parts were removed illegally from some abandoned machines. ([MyJoyOnline][5])

Eventually, many of these hulks were dismantled because they represented a long-term contamination hazard and a security problem. Some reports indicate that the remaining Mi-26 airframes at storage sites were eventually cut up for scrap in the 2010s. ([Reddit][6])

---

## Legacy

The Mi-26 crews at Chernobyl performed one of the most demanding helicopter operations in aviation history. They combined heavy-lift flying, precision cargo delivery, and operations in an intense radiation environment. The aircraft itself became symbolic of the enormous Soviet industrial response to the disaster: a machine designed to move tanks and heavy equipment was instead used as a tool in a nuclear emergency. ([Wikipedia][2])

The Chernobyl operation also demonstrated the limits of using conventional machinery in a nuclear accident. Many helicopters, vehicles, and machines that helped contain the disaster ultimately became contaminated artifacts of the liquidation effort rather than reusable equipment. ([Wikipedia][7])

# **About the Silent Witness of the Chernobyl Disaster**

*Posted by Oleg Khorenko – 27 April 2020* at <https://www.voenpens.club/blogs/3/1131/-?utm_source=chatgpt.com>

Yesterday, while rewatching the documentary *The Battle of Chernobyl* (Discovery Channel), I noticed a Mi-26 helicopter with the yellow tactical number **68** appearing briefly on screen. It reminded me of a few things, and I decided to share them with you. Perhaps someone else will also find them interesting. The written portion of this article will be fairly short, since there is very little information available, and what exists has been gathered from various sources across the Internet. If there are any inaccuracies, please do not judge too harshly.

This piece could probably be described as a **photo essay with captions and commentary**, dedicated to a single helicopter that took part in the liquidation of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant accident in 1986—a silent witness to those events, and one that I myself once had the opportunity to touch with my own hands.

So, let me introduce the subject:

**Mi-26 helicopter, yellow tactical number 68.**

* Factory serial number: **34001212025**
* Production number: **04-05**
* Built in **1984**
* Manufactured at the **Rostov Helicopter Plant (Rostvertol).**

### Source:

> "In 1990, following the reorganization of the **276th Separate Helicopter Regiment**, the regiment's **4th Helicopter Squadron** was disbanded. The Mi-26 helicopters were transferred to the **4th Squadron of the 65th Separate Helicopter Regiment** and redeployed to **Kobrin Airfield**.

At our school's training airfield (during the years I studied there), there were actually **two Mi-26 helicopters**, carrying the yellow tactical numbers **58** and **68**.

### From the recollections of LiveJournal user *safaniuk*:

> Cadets from two or three graduating classes spent countless hours climbing all over it.
>
> Even then we noticed that dandelions (the little yellow flowers) didn't grow beneath it.
>
> Later, the magazine *Ogonyok* published a photograph of that same Mi-26, tactical number 68, flying above the Chernobyl reactor.
>

As has already been rightly pointed out, this was simply due to a lack of knowledge. At the time, there was also a great deal of misunderstanding and fear.

However, it seems that his memories contain a small inaccuracy. He was most likely referring to aircraft **No. 58**, since the information quoted above shows that **No. 68 was not transferred until 1990**.

> Thus, these two helicopters had the honour of becoming the 'cleanest' examples and were transferred to KVATU."
