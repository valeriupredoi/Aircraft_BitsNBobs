# Mil Mi-26 of 276 Independent Aviation Regiment

- Yellow 66: not a Chernobyl bird
- Mil Mi-26 converted to Mi-26T
- factory serial number 34001212018
- tactical code 66 yellow(CCCP), 60 white(EW), RA-06286 (Scorpion Air, firefighter)
- probably not used during Chernobyl liquidation; The confusion likely comes from the fact that 34001212018 was a 276 OVP helicopter with bort (tail) number “66”. A list of Mi-26s in the regiment around 1988–1990 includes:
  - 34001212001 — bort 58
  - 34001212007 — bort 60
  - 34001212018 — bort 66
  - 34001212025 — bort 68

The helicopters sent to the radioactive waste burial site at Buryakovka after Chernobyl included aircraft such as:
- 34001212006 (bort 59)
- 34001212008 (bort 61)
- 34001212011 (bort 63)
- 34001212012 (bort 64)
- 34001212027 (bort 70)

Special case: 34001212025 (bort 68) — a Chernobyl aircraft, although it survived because it was reportedly considered relatively “clean” and was later used as a training airframe, being sent to scrap in 2009.

# **About the Silent Witness of the Chernobyl Disaster**

*Posted by Oleg Khorenko – 27 April 2020* at <https://www.voenpens.club/blogs/3/1131/-?utm_source=chatgpt.com>

Yesterday, while rewatching the documentary *The Battle of Chernobyl* (Discovery Channel), I noticed a Mi-26 helicopter with the yellow tactical number **68** appearing briefly on screen. It reminded me of a few things, and I decided to share them with you. Perhaps someone else will also find them interesting. The written portion of this article will be fairly short, since there is very little information available, and what exists has been gathered from various sources across the Internet. If there are any inaccuracies, please do not judge too harshly.

This piece could probably be described as a **photo essay with captions and commentary**, dedicated to a single helicopter that took part in the liquidation of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant accident in 1986—a silent witness to those events, and one that I myself once had the opportunity to touch with my own hands.

So, let me introduce the subject:

**Mi-26 helicopter, yellow tactical number 68.**

* Factory serial number: **34001212025**
* Production number: **04-05**
* Built in **1984**
* Manufactured at the **Rostov Helicopter Plant (Rostvertol).**

---

### Source:

> "In 1990, following the reorganization of the **276th Separate Helicopter Regiment**, the regiment's **4th Helicopter Squadron** was disbanded. The Mi-26 helicopters were transferred to the **4th Squadron of the 65th Separate Helicopter Regiment** and redeployed to **Kobrin Airfield**.
>
> One Mi-26—the 'cleanest' aircraft (yellow No. 68, factory number 34001212025)—was transferred as a training aid to the training airfield of the **Kirov Higher Military Aviation Technical School (KVATU)."

---

At our school's training airfield (during the years I studied there), there were actually **two Mi-26 helicopters**, carrying the yellow tactical numbers **58** and **68**.

### From the recollections of LiveJournal user *safaniuk*:

> "In 1987 a Mi-26 arrived at the Kirov Higher Military Aviation Technical School. Naturally, it was a major event! They hadn't even removed its VAP spray tanks (aviation liquid-dispensing equipment).
>
> Cadets from two or three graduating classes spent countless hours climbing all over it.
>
> Even then we noticed that dandelions (the little yellow flowers) didn't grow beneath it.
>
> Later, the magazine *Ogonyok* published a photograph of that same Mi-26, tactical number 68, flying above the Chernobyl reactor.
>
> It was then moved to the far corner of the airfield, and eventually dismantled and cut up."

As has already been rightly pointed out, this was simply due to a lack of knowledge. At the time, there was also a great deal of misunderstanding and fear.

However, it seems that his memories contain a small inaccuracy. He was most likely referring to aircraft **No. 58**, since the information quoted above shows that **No. 68 was not transferred until 1990**.

---

Another source states:

> "...the decision to transfer aircraft 58 and 68 to KVATU arose because the Mi-26 was still the newest helicopter in service, with relatively few flying hours. Normally it would have been impossible simply to hand such aircraft over to a school as training aids.
>
> But after the Chernobyl accident, the issue resolved itself.
>
> Thus, these two helicopters had the honour of becoming the 'cleanest' examples and were transferred to KVATU."

## 5.

Let's briefly step away from the main subject of this article.

This was another participant in the Chernobyl cleanup operation: **Mi-26, yellow No. 58**.

Because some of its major structural and mechanical components remained radioactive, cadets were not allowed to conduct training on it. It stood at the far end of the aircraft parking area on the edge of the airfield.

Nevertheless, I also had the opportunity to climb aboard it.

They say it was cut up for scrap metal in the late 1990s.

---

## 6.

It was aboard **aircraft No. 68** that the cadets at our school—including myself—learned to operate and maintain the magnificent Mi-26 helicopter, developing our practical skills in aircraft maintenance.

---

## 7.

Incidentally, both Mi-26 helicopters—Nos. **58 and 68**—flew to our training airfield under their own power.

But neither of them would ever take to the skies again.

In the photograph:

My classmate **Alexander M.** (who is still serving and continues to fly the Mi-26 today) and myself during maintenance training on Mi-26 No. 68 at the **Kirov Higher Military Aviation Technical School**, 1993.

---

## 8.

But everything eventually comes to an end, and our main character was no exception.

Indeed, its story also came to an end.

> "On 30 September 2008, mixed scrap metal from the demilitarized and condemned Mi-26 airframe, factory number 34001212025, military unit 22274, Kirov, was offered for sale.
>
> The helicopter was scrapped in 2009."

---

And with that, I shall conclude this short photographic account of a worthy representative of Mikhail Mil's family of rotary-wing aircraft—the Mi-26 helicopter carrying yellow tactical number **68**.

It will forever remain in our memories, in photographs, and in those few surviving pieces of film footage.

### Notes

This article contains a few interesting historical points:

* It identifies **Mi-26 "Yellow 68" (factory no. 34001212025)** as one of the helicopters involved in the Chernobyl cleanup.
* It states that **Yellow 68 was regarded as one of the "cleanest" surviving aircraft**, making it suitable for transfer to the Kirov aviation technical school as a training airframe.
* It also distinguishes **Yellow 58**, noting that some of its powerplant components still exhibited elevated radiation levels and that cadets were therefore not trained on it.
* The author concludes that the LiveJournal recollection placing No. 68 at the school in 1987 is probably mistaken, since documentary evidence indicates it was transferred only in **1990** after the reorganization of the **276th Separate Helicopter Regiment**.

It's a fascinating article because it's one of the few firsthand accounts linking specific Mi-26 airframes to both the Chernobyl operation and their later use as instructional aircraft.
